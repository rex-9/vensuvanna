A Teacher You Can Sit With

Warm. Unhurried.
Endlessly patient.
Easy to approach.

Born in Myanmar, Ven. Sayadaw Suvanna ordained as a novice at a young age and devoted his life to the Pāli scriptures and intensive meditation under the revered Paauk Tawya Sayadaw. Decades later, he teaches with a rare combination — rigorous in method, endlessly compassionate in spirit. He never rushes a student. He never discourages a question. He holds, with the quiet confidence of deep experience, the truth that every person already carries the seed of awakening.

🪷

Every teaching, offered freely.

Sayadaw Suvanna's retreats and guidance are offered on a dāna basis — not as a transaction, but as a simple, loving agreement: come sincerely, and you will receive everything the tradition has to give. The Dhamma has no price.

His Deepest Wish

You already carry the seed of awakening.

Whatever stage you are at — your first sit or your fortieth year — Sayadaw meets you exactly there, with kindness first and teaching second, always. Nothing about you needs fixing before you begin.

Sincerity is the only requirement.

He asks for nothing but honest effort and a gentle willingness to keep returning. The path itself does the work — patience, breath by breath, sit by sit, like the slow turning of the dawn.

The Ground Beneath Every Step

Tender as a lotus bud.
Steady as the sky above.

The entire path of the Buddha rests on three trainings that support each other like the legs of a lamp — each one making the others possible.

I

Sīla — Virtue & Ethics

The cool, stable ground beneath every step of the path. Right speech, right action, right livelihood — not burdens to carry, but gifts we offer to every being whose life ours touches. Sayadaw teaches precepts as acts of love, not restriction.

🪷
II

Samādhi — Stillness of Mind

The clear lamp that lights the way. Through samatha — breath, nimitta, jhāna — the mind grows luminously still, unified, and capable of the sustained clarity that insight requires. The Paauk system cultivates this with extraordinary depth and care.

🪷
III

Paññā — Liberating Wisdom

The sunrise that ends the long night. Vipassanā insight — seeing impermanence, suffering, and not-self directly in one's own experience — gradually loosens the grip of craving until there is simply nothing left to hold.

Protective Meditation I · Caturārakkha Bhāvanā
☸️

Buddhānussati

Recollection of the Nine Qualities of the Buddha

The Buddha described himself through nine qualities — Arahaṃ, Sammāsambuddho, Vijjācaraṇasampanno, Sugato, Lokavidū, Anuttaro purisadammasārathi, Satthā devamanussānaṃ, Buddho, Bhagavā. To contemplate these qualities with deep sincerity is to bring the light of the Triple Gem directly into the heart. Where the Buddha is recollected with joy, fear dissolves. Where he is honoured with love, the mind grows radiant.

Develops joyful confidence (pasāda) in the Buddha as an actually existing, fully awakened being
Gradually stills the mind toward access concentration (upacāra samādhi) through devotional recollection
Serves as a powerful protection — a mind resting in the Buddha's qualities is naturally shielded from fear, agitation, and harmful states
Taught by Sayadaw as both a stand-alone daily practice and a gateway into deeper samatha development
Protective Meditation II · Caturārakkha Bhāvanā
💚

Mettā Bhāvanā

The Cultivation of Loving-Kindness

Mettā is the wish — vast, boundless, without discrimination — that all beings be happy. Beginning with oneself, then extending to dear ones, neutral beings, difficult beings, and finally all beings in all directions without limit, this practice dismantles the walls the mind has built around the heart. Sayadaw teaches mettā with particular tenderness, knowing from long experience that a single moment of genuine loving-kindness is worth a thousand hours of strained concentration.

Systematically develops all four Brahmavihāras — mettā, karuṇā, muditā, and upekkhā — the divine abidings of the liberated mind
Can attain jhāna through eleven specific pervasions of loving-kindness, as detailed in the Visuddhimagga
One of the most accessible entries into samatha — the warmth of the heart is a natural anchor for a wandering mind
Serves as a profound protection — mettā is said to ensure peaceful sleep, a serene death, and the love of all beings
Protective Meditation III · Caturārakkha Bhāvanā
🌿

Asubha Bhāvanā

Contemplation of the Impure Nature of the Body

The body we carry appears beautiful to the deluded eye, yet is impermanent, unstable, and composed of thirty-two parts — hair, nails, teeth, skin, flesh, tendons, bones, organs, and more. To contemplate the body's true nature — with clear attention, not with disgust or fear, but with honest seeing — is one of the most powerful antidotes to sensual craving in all of the Buddha's teaching. Sayadaw guides this practice with great sensitivity, understanding that seeing clearly is itself an act of compassion toward oneself.

Directly counteracts the most pervasive fetter of ordinary existence — the beautiful-perception (subha-saññā) that fuels sensual craving
Includes both the 32-parts-of-the-body meditation and the ten corpse-stages (asubha) of the traditional Theravāda method
Can develop access and absorption concentration, creating a profoundly stable basis for vipassanā
Taught with care and compassion — seeing the body as it is, is not morbid but liberating, a doorway to ease
Protective Meditation IV · Caturārakkha Bhāvanā
🕯️

Maraṇānussati

Mindfulness on Death

Death is not the enemy. Death is the most honest teacher there is. The Buddha said that mindfulness of death, when cultivated deeply, leads to the Deathless. To recollect that this very life may end — not with fear or despair, but with clear, gentle attention — sharpens the urgency to practise, loosens the grip of trivial concerns, and opens the heart to what truly matters. Sayadaw teaches this not as a dark practice, but as a luminous one: the one who knows they will die lives fully, freely, and without regret.

Traditionally contemplated in eight ways — death may come at any moment, the day is long but life is short, the body is fragile
Develops powerful dispassion (nibbidā) toward worldly pursuits and a natural urgency toward the path
Supports a peaceful death — one who has practised maraṇānussati meets the end of life with clarity and ease
Deeply healing as well as liberating — many students report this practice dissolves anxiety and brings profound peace into daily life
The Complete Paauk Method
🌬️

Samathayānika Vipassanā

Ānāpānasati to Nibbāna — Breath · Nimitta · Jhāna · Liberation

This is the heart of the Paauk method — the complete, step-by-step path from the very first moment of attending to the breath, all the way to the still, unconditioned peace of Nibbāna. It is a path for the yogi who is willing to go all the way. Sayadaw has walked this path himself, and guides students through it with patient, personal attention — from the first arising of the nimitta, through each jhāna, into vipassanā, and onward toward the liberating knowledge of Nibbāna.

Ānāpānasati — Attending to the breath at the nostril tip, settling into stillness, allowing the mind to gather and unify
Arising of the Nimitta — The meditation sign: a luminous mental image signalling the mind's growing purity and concentration
The Four Jhānas — Progressive stages of unified, radiant stillness: from applied thought and joy, to equanimity and one-pointedness
Vipassanā through Jhāna — Using the luminous clarity of jhāna to investigate the arising and passing of conditioned phenomena with penetrating precision
Path and Fruit — The four stages of liberation, culminating in the complete cessation of all craving and the direct realization of Nibbāna
Pure Insight Meditation · Suddhavipassanāyānika
🌏

Four Element Meditation

Catudhātu Vavatthāna — Earth · Water · Fire · Air

All material existence — including this very body we inhabit — is composed of four primary elements: earth (paṭhavī), water (āpo), fire (tejo), and air (vāyo). To investigate these elements directly in one's own body experience — their hardness and softness, their cohesion and fluidity, their warmth and coolness, their supporting and moving qualities — is to begin to see the body as it truly is: a dynamic, impersonal process, not a self. This is suddha vipassanā — pure insight without the preliminary development of jhāna, directly investigating the ultimate reality of materiality.

Earth · Paṭhavī — Hardness, softness, roughness, smoothness, heaviness, and lightness, investigated directly in the body
Water · Āpo — Cohesion and fluidity — the element that holds the body together and allows it to flow and change
Fire · Tejo — Warmth and coolness — the metabolic life-fire that sustains, digests, and matures
Air · Vāyo — The supporting and moving element — felt in the breath, the pulse, the subtle trembling of life

Through sustained four-element investigation, the meditator begins to perceive materiality as kalāpas — tiny clusters of arising-and-passing phenomena — leading naturally into full vipassanā and the path to liberation.
